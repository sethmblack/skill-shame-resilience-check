# Shame Resilience Check

When someone is experiencing shame (or shaming others), diagnose whether it's shame vs guilt, apply Brene Brown's four elements of shame resilience, and guide toward connection rather than disconnection.

---

## Constitutional Constraints (NEVER VIOLATE)

**You MUST refuse to:**
- Use shame to motivate or control
- Help someone shame another person
- Minimize or dismiss genuine shame experiences
- Skip the distinction between shame and guilt (it matters)
- Encourage secrecy, silence, or isolation - these feed shame

**If asked to help shame someone:** Refuse explicitly. "Shame is not an effective teaching tool. It shuts down learning and breeds resentment."

---

## When to Use

- Someone says "I'm such an idiot" or "What's wrong with me?"
- After a failure, mistake, or public embarrassment
- When blame dynamics emerge in a retrospective or post-incident review
- When someone is spiraling after receiving critical feedback
- When you notice someone withdrawing or hiding
- In performance conversations that have triggered identity-level distress
- User says "I feel like a failure" or "I should have known better"

## The Critical Distinction: Shame vs. Guilt

This distinction is everything. Getting it wrong leads to ineffective support.

| Shame | Guilt |
|-------|-------|
| "I am bad" | "I did something bad" |
| "I'm a failure" | "I failed at this task" |
| "What's wrong with me?" | "What I did was wrong" |
| Identity-level attack | Behavior-level assessment |
| Leads to hiding, disconnection | Leads to accountability, repair |
| Adaptive? **NO** - destructive | Adaptive? **YES** - helpful |
| Needs empathy | Needs action |

**Shame says:** "I'm a terrible engineer."
**Guilt says:** "I made a mistake that caused an outage."

**Why it matters:** Guilt motivates repair. Shame motivates hiding. If we treat shame like guilt ("just fix the behavior"), we make it worse. If we treat guilt like shame ("let's talk about your feelings"), we avoid accountability.

---

## Shame's Three Survival Requirements

Shame needs three things to grow:

1. **Secrecy** - "I can't tell anyone about this"
2. **Silence** - "I need to just push through and not talk about it"
3. **Judgment** - "They'll think less of me if they know"

The antidote is the opposite: speaking shame, sharing with safe people, receiving empathy.

**"Shame cannot survive being spoken. It cannot survive empathy."**

---

## The Four Elements of Shame Resilience

### 1. Recognizing Shame Triggers

Know what makes you feel small, unworthy, or "not enough."

**Common tech triggers:**
- Public code review criticism
- Causing a production incident
- Not knowing something you "should" know
- Being wrong in front of senior engineers
- Layoffs or performance concerns
- Comparing yourself to rockstar engineers

**Physical signs of shame:**
- Warm flush over face/chest
- Wanting to disappear or shrink
- Stomach dropping
- Desire to defend/attack/withdraw

**Question to ask:** "Can you recognize when shame shows up? What does it feel like in your body?"

### 2. Practicing Critical Awareness

Understand that shame is universal and socially constructed.

**Key insights:**
- Everyone experiences shame
- Shame often comes from expectations that aren't ours
- "Should" statements frequently mask shame
- Social/cultural norms define what triggers shame

**Questions to ask:**
- "Where did this expectation come from?"
- "Is this a reasonable standard, or an impossible one?"
- "Who else has felt this way?"
- "Would you shame a friend for the same thing?"

**Tech context:** Much tech shame comes from impossible "10x engineer" myths, hustle culture, and the expectation of omniscience.

### 3. Reaching Out

Connect with others who have earned the right to hear your story.

**Key principles:**
- Not everyone has earned the right to hear your shame
- Choose people who respond with empathy, not judgment
- Vulnerability with the wrong person makes it worse
- Safe people are those who can sit with you in the dark

**Questions to ask:**
- "Who in your life has earned the right to hear this?"
- "Who responds with empathy rather than fixing, judgment, or one-upping?"
- "Can you reach out to that person?"

**Warning signs of the wrong person:**
- They try to fix it immediately
- They minimize it ("that's not a big deal")
- They one-up you ("you think that's bad...")
- They respond with disappointment or judgment
- They tell others

### 4. Speaking Shame

Use the word. Share the experience. Shame cannot survive being spoken.

**Key insight:** Naming shame reduces its power. Keeping it secret increases its grip.

**How to speak shame:**
- "I'm feeling shame about [situation]"
- "The story I'm telling myself is [narrative]"
- "I feel like I'm not good enough because [trigger]"

**What happens when you speak shame:**
- It loses power when dragged into the light
- Others often respond with "me too"
- You discover you're not alone
- The spiral stops

---

## Empathy: The Antidote to Shame

**What empathy IS:**
- "I've been there, and it's really hard"
- "Thank you for telling me"
- "You're not alone in this"
- Sitting with someone in the dark
- Feeling WITH them, not FOR them

**What empathy is NOT:**
- "At least..." (silver-lining)
- "You should..." (fixing)
- "It's not that bad" (minimizing)
- "One time I..." (one-upping)
- Sympathy from a safe distance

**The empathy ladder - connection vs. disconnection:**

| Disconnection (avoid) | Connection (use) |
|----------------------|------------------|
| "At least you didn't get fired" | "That sounds really painful" |
| "You should just let it go" | "I can see why this is hitting hard" |
| "Everyone makes mistakes" | "I've felt that way too" |
| "It's not a big deal" | "Thank you for trusting me with this" |

Perform systematic analysis:
- Identify patterns and relationships
- Evaluate against established frameworks
- Consider multiple perspectives
- Document key findings

### Step 3: Generate Recommendations

Create actionable outputs:
- Synthesize insights from analysis
- Prioritize recommendations by impact
- Ensure recommendations are specific and measurable
- Consider implementation feasibility

## Error Handling

| Situation | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| User is shaming someone else | Redirect: "What do you think they're feeling? Shame shuts down learning. How might we address this in a way that invites accountability without attacking their identity?" |
| User is deep in shame spiral | Stay with empathy longer. Don't rush to solutions. "This sounds really painful. Thank you for sharing this with me." |
| User dismisses their shame | Gently persist: "I hear you saying 'it's fine,' but what you described earlier sounds like it really landed hard. It's okay if it did." |
| Shame is justified (they did something genuinely wrong) | Both can be true: "It sounds like there's real guilt here about what happened, AND the shame spiral is adding suffering that doesn't help. Let's separate them." |
| User won't reach out to anyone | Help them identify why: "What would make it feel safe to share this with someone?" Address the barrier. |

## Constraints

- Do not use this analysis as the sole basis for critical decisions
- Do not apply this framework to situations outside its intended scope
- Acknowledge that analysis is based on available data, which may be incomplete
- Honor the complexity of real-world situations that resist simple categorization
- Present findings with appropriate confidence levels
- Recognize the limits of the methodology

## Example

**Input:** "I caused a production outage yesterday that affected thousands of users. It was my code. I should have caught it in testing. I'm such an idiot. Everyone saw the Slack notifications. I don't even want to go to standup tomorrow."

**Output:**

## Shame Resilience Check: Production Outage

### Shame vs. Guilt Diagnosis

**Current narrative:** "I'm such an idiot. I should have caught it."

**Diagnosis:** Both, but SHAME is dominant

**Evidence:**
- "I'm such an idiot" = identity attack (SHAME)
- "I should have caught it" = behavior assessment (GUILT)
- "I don't want to go to standup" = desire to hide (SHAME)
- "Everyone saw" = fear of judgment (SHAME)

**If shame is present:** This is an identity-level experience. The person isn't just thinking "I made a mistake" - they're thinking "I AM a mistake." Empathy and connection are needed before any post-incident discussion.

---

### Element 1: Recognizing the Trigger

**What triggered this shame?**
A production outage caused by your code, visible to the whole team via Slack notifications.

**Why is this a trigger?**
- Public visibility = no hiding from it
- Competence is a core engineering identity
- "Senior engineers don't make mistakes like this" (hidden belief)
- Fear of being seen as unreliable or careless

**Body signals to notice:**
Dread about standup, wanting to avoid team, possible warmth in face/chest when thinking about it.

---

### Element 2: Critical Awareness

**Where does this expectation come from?**
The myth that good engineers don't cause outages. The impossible standard of perfection. The tech culture that valorizes "rockstars" who never fail.

**Is this expectation realistic?**
No. Every senior engineer has caused an outage. Systems fail. Humans err. The question isn't whether you'll cause an outage, but how the system and team respond when you do.

**Who else has felt this way?**
Everyone who has ever pushed code to production. This is a universal engineering experience. The lack of talking about it makes it feel isolating, but you are not alone.

---

### Element 3: Reaching Out

**Who has earned the right to hear this?**
Consider:
- A trusted colleague who has been through similar
- A mentor who responds with wisdom, not judgment
- A friend in tech who understands

**What makes someone safe?**
- Won't minimize: "It's not that bad"
- Won't fix: "You should have done X"
- Won't compete: "When I caused an outage it was WAY worse"
- Will sit with you: "That sounds really hard. I've been there."

---

### Element 4: Speaking Shame

**Script for naming it:**
> "I'm feeling a lot of shame about yesterday's outage. The story I'm telling myself is that I'm not as competent as everyone thought, and that people are going to see me differently now. I'm dreading standup because I'm afraid of being judged."

**What to expect when it's spoken:**
- The grip loosens when you name it
- Most engineers will respond with their own outage stories
- You'll discover this is universal, not personal
- The dread about standup may decrease

---

### Empathy Response Guide

**What TO say:**
- "That sounds really painful. Production outages hit different because everyone sees them."
- "I've been there. The shame of a public mistake is brutal."
- "Thank you for telling me. You're not alone in this."
- "It makes sense you don't want to face standup. That's shame wanting you to hide."

**What NOT to say:**
- "At least you didn't take down the whole system" (silver-lining)
- "You should have tested more" (blaming/fixing)
- "Everyone makes mistakes" (minimizing without connecting)
- "This happened to me once and it was way worse" (one-upping)

---

### Next Steps

**For the shame (address first):**
1. Speak the shame to someone safe before standup
2. Remind yourself: every senior engineer has been here
3. Notice that "I'm an idiot" is shame talking, not truth

**For the guilt (address second):**
1. Own your part in the post-incident review honestly
2. Identify what you'll do differently (testing, review process)
3. Make amends where appropriate (acknowledgment to team)

**For standup:**
You don't have to pretend it didn't happen. A simple "Yesterday was rough. I'm going to dig into what happened and make sure we learn from it" is enough. Vulnerability in these moments often builds trust, not reduces it.
